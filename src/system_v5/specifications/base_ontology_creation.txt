1. The DUL Foundation Ontology (DOLCE+DnS Ultralite)
What it is:
DUL is a lightweight foundational (upper-level) ontology. It is a simplified and highly pragmatic version of the more complex DOLCE Lite-Plus library combined with the Constructive Descriptions and Situations (cDnS) framework.

Its Purpose:
It provides a foundational modeling framework for representing both physical and social contexts. Instead of forcing restrictive philosophical commitments, it gives ontology designers an easy-to-apply vocabulary to map out concepts such as Physical Objects, Social Entities, Events, and Information without suffering from typical semantic ambiguities (e.g., distinguishing between a piece of information and the physical medium it's printed on).

By the Numbers (in raw_dul.txt):

Classes (owl:Class): 93
Object Properties (owl:ObjectProperty): 113
Datatype Properties (owl:DatatypeProperty): 5
Subclass Axioms (rdfs:subClassOf): ~77 explicit taxonomic assertions.
Size: Over 2,700 lines of complex RDF/Turtle triples with exhaustive comments, disjointness statements, and nested property chains.
2. How You Approached and Adapted It
Your version aggressively "prunes and flattens" the bloated OWL ontology into an incredibly streamlined, LLM-executable semantic graph. Here is an analysis of your engineering choices in deriving your version from raw_dul.txt:

A. Radical Dimensionality Reduction (93 Classes 
→
→ 14 Classes)
Instead of keeping granular, edge-case classes like Personification, DesignedArtifact, or FormalEntity, you extracted only the absolute foundational pillars.

Your classes.txt boils the world down to 14 core nodes: Entity, Object, PhysicalObject, SocialObject, InformationObject, InformationRealization, Event, Action, Quality, Concept, Role, Collection, TimeInterval, and SpaceRegion.
You maintained DUL’s most powerful philosophical advantage: the strict split between Physical Entities, Social Entities, and Information Realizations (which is critical for agent loop environments).
B. Property Simplification (113+ Relations 
→
→ 25 Core Axioms)
In DUL, relationships are entangled in complex hierarchies (e.g., hasComponent is a sub-property of hasPart, directlyFollows sub-properties, etc.).

Your axioms.txt extracts exactly 25 actionable relations (like hasPart, realizes, classifies, isAbout, satisfies).
You stripped away all the OWL-specific inverse pathing logic, property chains, and transitive requirements, replacing them with flat, self-contained definitions.
C. Conversion to Agent-Readable JSON-like Arrays

From Semantic Web to Software: raw_dul.txt is written in Turtle (.ttl/.owl), which is highly inefficient for LLM prompt context windows due to prefixing and syntax boilerplate.
Your Format: You serialized the concepts into an explicit array of JSON objects ([ {"class": "...", "description": "..."} ]).
Explicit Domains and Ranges: In OWL, rdfs:domain and rdfs:range are computationally inferred. In your axioms.txt, you explicitly hardcoded the allowable domains and ranges as string arrays for each relation (e.g., precedes is strictly constrained to ["Event", "Action", "TimeInterval"]).
Summary:
You took an academic Semantic Web standard and successfully translated it into a deterministic, prompt-injected JSON schema. By removing 85% of the ontology's philosophical bloat, you retained the robust upper-ontology taxonomy of DUL but adapted it perfectly for an LLM to quickly parse, classify data against, and generate knowledge graphs without hallucinating out-of-bounds relationships.
